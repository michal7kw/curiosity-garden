<h1>The Feynman Technique</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Named after Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman, this technique is a powerful method for learning anything deeply. The core idea: if you can't explain something simply, you don't understand it well enough. By teaching a concept as if explaining to a child, you identify gaps in your knowledge and force genuine understanding.</p>
<h2>Key Principles</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Choose a concept</strong>: Pick something you want to learn or understand better</li>
<li><strong>Explain it simply</strong>: Write an explanation as if teaching a 12-year-oldâ€”no jargon, just simple language</li>
<li><strong>Identify gaps</strong>: Notice where your explanation breaks down or feels unclear</li>
<li><strong>Review and simplify</strong>: Go back to sources, fill gaps, and make your explanation even simpler</li>
</ol>
<h2>Examples</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Learning physics</strong>: Feynman famously used simple analogies to explain quantum mechanics</li>
<li><strong>Mastering programming</strong>: Explaining how a function works to a rubber duck (rubber duck debugging)</li>
<li><strong>Understanding business concepts</strong>: Breaking down complex financial instruments into everyday language</li>
</ul>
<h2>Mental Models</h2>
<p>This technique embodies:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong><a href="/garden/dev_admin/insight/teaching-forces-deeper-understanding/" class="internal-link">Teaching Forces Deeper Understanding</a></strong>: The act of explaining consolidates knowledge</li>
<li><strong><a href="/garden/dev_admin/concept/first-principles-thinking/" class="internal-link">First Principles Thinking</a></strong>: You naturally break down concepts to basics when simplifying</li>
<li><strong>Active processing</strong>: Transforms passive knowledge into active understanding</li>
</ul>
<h2>Common Misconceptions</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Not about dumbing down</strong>: Simplification requires deeper understanding, not less</li>
<li><strong>Works for complex topics</strong>: The harder the concept, the more valuable this technique</li>
<li><strong>Not just verbal</strong>: Writing explanations is often even more effective</li>
</ul>
